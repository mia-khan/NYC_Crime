4. Park Size vs. Crime Count

Scatter plot analyzing the relationship between park size (acres) and total crime count. This scatter plot explores whether there‚Äôs a correlation between the size of a park and the amount of crime that occurs within it. Each point represents a park, with colors indicating the borough.

Analysis Details

  • The visualization reveals that while there is a general trend of larger parks having more crime incidents, the relationship is not strictly linear.
  • Several outliers show that some large parks have disproportionately high crime counts, while others maintain relatively low numbers despite their size.
  • The clustering of points by borough suggests that location may play a more significant role than size alone in determining crime rates.
  • Smaller parks (under 50 acres) show considerable variance in crime counts, indicating that factors beyond size (such as location, design, and usage patterns) significantly influence safety.

This analysis helps urban planners understand that size alone is not deterministic of park safety, and that design elements and location context must be considered in security planning.

Interpreting Outliers

While the scatter plot shows a general trend where larger parks tend to have more crime incidents, several outliers suggest that size is not the only factor. We identified outliers as having over 40 in crime counts and 1000 acres in size. They are also annotated in red. For example:

  • Large parks with low crime may benefit from less foot traffic, better surveillance, or isolated locations.
  • Small parks with high crime counts ‚Äî especially in densely populated boroughs like Manhattan ‚Äî likely reflect high usage, central locations, or limited visibility.

These deviations indicate that crime in parks is influenced not just by area but also by social, geographic, and environmental context.
